# LLM Memory and Knowledge: A 2025–2026 Research Map Across Four Threads

## TL;DR
- The field has decisively split memory into two regimes — **parametric** (knowledge baked into weights) and **contextual/non-parametric** (knowledge supplied at inference) — and the 2025–2026 consensus is that the contextual/external side (RAG + agent memory) is where the most usable progress is happening, while the parametric side (mechanistic storage + weight editing) is increasingly seen as scientifically rich but practically fragile.
- The "just use long context" thesis has been empirically rejected for production: 2025 work on **context rot** (Chroma) and persistent **lost-in-the-middle** effects show accuracy degrades well before the window fills, so the emerging default is **hybrid** (retrieve, then long-context-reason) rather than either extreme.
- For **portable, model-agnostic memory** (Claude/Gemini/Cursor), the evidence favors **natural-language/structured memory stores surfaced over MCP** rather than raw-embedding stores, because embeddings are model-specific and non-portable while text is universal; weight-editing approaches are inherently non-portable and unsuited to cross-platform memory.

## Key Findings
1. **Mechanistic storage is real but not cleanly "localizable."** The FFN-as-key-value-memory lineage (Geva 2021 → ROME/Knowledge Neurons 2022) gave way to a serious 2023–2026 critique: Hase et al. showed edit success is nearly uncorrelated with where causal tracing localizes a fact, and superposition/knowledge-circuit work shows facts are distributed and entangled. Allen-Zhu & Li's ~2 bits/parameter capacity law (ICLR 2025) is the most cited quantitative anchor.
2. **RAG did not die; it matured into "context engineering."** The 2025–2026 frontier is agentic RAG, GraphRAG, self-correcting retrieval (Self-RAG/CRAG), and late-interaction multi-vector retrieval (ColBERT family), plus graph/neurobiological memory (HippoRAG).
3. **Knowledge editing has hit a wall at scale.** Sequential/lifelong editing reliably causes catastrophic forgetting and model collapse; null-space methods (AlphaEdit, ICLR 2025 Outstanding Paper) and ripple-effect benchmarks (RippleEdits) define the current state, and several groups now argue in-context/retrieval-based updating beats parameter editing.
4. **Agent memory is the most active applied area.** MemGPT/Letta, Generative Agents, Mem0/Mem0g, A-MEM, and RL-based managers (Memory-R1) anchor a fast-moving space evaluated on LoCoMo/LongMemEval, with 2026 surveys formalizing a write–manage–read loop.

## Details

### Thread 1 — Mechanistic Storage: How and Where Knowledge Lives in Weights

**Foundational lineage.** The modern story starts with **Geva et al., "Transformer Feed-Forward Layers Are Key-Value Memories" (EMNLP 2021)**, which reframed FFN layers as key-value memories where keys detect input patterns and values induce output distributions. **Meng et al., "Locating and Editing Factual Associations in GPT" (ROME, NeurIPS 2022)** introduced *causal tracing* (representation denoising) to localize factual recall to mid-layer MLPs at the last subject token, then performed rank-one edits. In parallel, **Dai et al., "Knowledge Neurons in Pretrained Transformers" (ACL 2022)** used integrated gradients to attribute facts to individual neurons. **Allen-Zhu & Li, "Physics of Language Models: Part 3.3, Knowledge Capacity Scaling Laws" (arXiv 2404.05405; ICLR 2025)** established the field's key quantitative anchor; verbatim, "language models can and only can store **2 bits of knowledge per parameter, even when quantized to int8**... Consequently, a 7B model can store 14B bits of knowledge, surpassing the English Wikipedia and textbooks combined based on our estimation." Their companion "Part 3.x" papers studied knowledge extraction and manipulation.

**The localization critique (the central debate).** The clean "locate-then-edit" picture has been substantially destabilized:
- **Hase et al., "Does Localization Inform Editing?" (NeurIPS 2023)** found that edit success is **essentially unrelated** to where causal tracing localizes a fact — the correlation between ROME rewrite score and tracing effect at layer 6 of GPT-J was slightly *negative* (ρ ≈ −0.13). The layer you edit predicts success far better than where the fact "is."
- **Geva et al. (2023)** and subsequent work showed factual association is distributed across many parameters/layers, not a single locus.
- **"Knowledge in Superposition: Unveiling the Failures of Lifelong Knowledge Editing" (arXiv 2408.07413)** derived mathematically that lifelong editing introduces an interference term rooted in superposition — different facts are stored *nearly* (not perfectly) orthogonally due to capacity limits, guaranteeing eventual forgetting.
- **Yao et al., "Knowledge Circuits in Pretrained Transformers" (arXiv 2405.17969, NeurIPS 2024)** reframed knowledge as living in sparse computational subgraphs ("knowledge circuits") spanning MLPs and attention, not isolated weights.

**Linear representation hypothesis (LRH) and its refinements.** **Park, Choe & Veitch, "The Linear Representation Hypothesis and the Geometry of Large Language Models" (ICML 2024)** formalized concepts as linear directions and introduced a causal inner product. 2024–2025 work both extended and challenged it: **Engels et al., "Not All Language Model Features Are Linear" (arXiv 2405.14860)** found irreducible multi-dimensional features (e.g., circular representations of days/months); **Csordás et al.** found nonlinear "onion" representations in RNNs. Anthropic's interpretability program (Toy Models of Superposition; sparse autoencoders/dictionary learning; "Circuits Updates" 2024–2025) is the leading industrial effort, now grappling with cross-layer and attention superposition.

**Critiques of reverse-engineering.** A notable 2025 development: prominent researchers (Sharkey et al. 2025 open-problems survey; Hendrycks; Nanda) have voiced skepticism that current localization/SAE methods illuminate *verifiable, behavior-causing* mechanisms, with some arguing against continued naive reverse-engineering. MoE models add a new localization frontier (e.g., cross-lingual inconsistency-based localization).

**Open problems:** whether facts are localizable at all in a useful sense; how superposition limits editing; whether SAEs recover causal features; reconciling the ~2 bits/param law with distributed circuits. **Leading groups:** David Bau's lab (Northeastern), Mor Geva / Amir Globerson (Tel Aviv/Google), Anthropic interpretability, Meta FAIR (Allen-Zhu), Peter Hase / Mohit Bansal (UNC), Kiho Park / Victor Veitch (Chicago).

### Thread 2 — Retrieval Systems (Non-Parametric Knowledge Injection)

**Foundational lineage.** **kNN-LM (Khandelwal et al., ICLR 2020)** interpolated LM predictions with nearest-neighbor lookups over a datastore. **REALM (Guu et al., 2020)** and **DPR (Karpukhin et al., EMNLP 2020)** introduced learned dense retrieval; **RAG (Lewis et al., NeurIPS 2020)** married a dense retriever to a generator. **RETRO (Borgeaud et al., 2022)** showed retrieval from trillions of tokens can match much larger models. **FiD (Izacard & Grave)** and **Atlas (Izacard et al.)** advanced fusion-in-decoder and few-shot retrieval-augmented learning.

**2025–2026 advances.**
- **Agentic RAG:** The 2025 "year of agentic AI" reframed retrieval as an autonomous decision loop where the model decides *when/what/how* to retrieve (surveyed by Singh et al. 2025). Methods include ReAct-style retrieval, **Self-RAG (Asai et al., ICLR 2024)** — reflection tokens (IsREL/IsSUP/IsUSE) for on-demand retrieval and self-critique — and RL-trained search agents (Search-R1, Search-o1, DeepResearcher).
- **Self-correcting retrieval:** **CRAG (Yan et al., 2024)** adds a lightweight T5-based retrieval evaluator that triggers Correct/Incorrect/Ambiguous actions and web search fallback; it's plug-and-play atop RAG and Self-RAG.
- **GraphRAG:** **Microsoft's "From Local to Global: A Graph RAG Approach to Query-Focused Summarization" (Edge, Trinh, Cheng, Bradley, Chao, Mody, Truitt, Metropolitansky, Ness, Larson; arXiv 2404.16130, 2024)** builds an entity knowledge graph, applies Leiden community detection, and pre-summarizes communities for global "sensemaking" queries; it reports comprehensiveness win rates of 72–83% over vector RAG while needing "97% fewer tokens" for root-level community summaries. **HippoRAG (Gutiérrez, Shu, Gu, Yasunaga, Su; NeurIPS 2024; arXiv 2405.14831)** uses Personalized PageRank over an open KG (hippocampal-indexing analogy) for single-step multi-hop retrieval, outperforming prior RAG "by up to 20%" while being 10–20× cheaper/6–13× faster than iterative retrieval; **HippoRAG 2 ("From RAG to Memory," Gutiérrez et al., ICML 2025, arXiv 2502.14802)** extends it for non-parametric continual learning, reporting a 7% gain on associative-memory tasks over a strong embedding model.
- **Late-interaction / multi-vector retrieval:** ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, SIGIR 2020) and **ColBERTv2 (Santhanam et al., NAACL 2022)** with PLAID indexing; 2024–2025 extensions include Jina-ColBERT-v2 (multilingual, MRL 2024), ColPali/Video-ColBERT (multimodal), and serving optimizations (ColBERT-serve). A first **Workshop on Late Interaction and Multi-Vector Retrieval (ECIR 2026)** signals field maturation. (Note: reproducibility studies report multi-vector models drop 86–97% on long narrative queries, so late interaction is not a universal win.)
- **Hybrid / adaptive:** LDAR (distraction-aware retrieval, arXiv 2509.21865), Adaptive-RAG (complexity-routed depth), and Self-Route (route between RAG and long context).

**Open problems:** trusted evidence ranking at scale, RAG security (SafeRAG attack classes), structured/multimodal fusion, and retrieval for personalization. **Leading groups:** AI2 / UW (Asai, Hajishirzi), Stanford (Khattab, Zaharia, Manning), Microsoft Research (GraphRAG), Ohio State NLP (Su, Gutiérrez), Meta FAIR (Izacard, Lewis).

### Thread 3 — Knowledge Editing

**Foundational lineage.** **De Cao et al., "Editing Factual Knowledge in Language Models" (KnowledgeEditor, EMNLP 2021)** used a hypernetwork to predict weight updates. **Mitchell et al.** contributed **MEND (ICLR 2022)** (gradient-decomposition meta-learning) and **SERAC (ICML 2022)** (memory-based editing with a separate counterfactual model — a non-parametric approach). **Meng et al., MEMIT, "Mass-Editing Memory in a Transformer" (ICLR 2023)** scaled ROME's closed-form rank-one update to thousands of edits across multiple layers.

**2025–2026 developments.**
- **Lifelong/sequential editing & its failures:** Editing at scale causes **gradual then catastrophic forgetting** and model collapse (Gupta et al., "Model Editing at Scale Leads to Gradual and Catastrophic Forgetting," ACL Findings 2024). **AlphaEdit (Fang et al., arXiv 2410.02355; ICLR 2025 Outstanding Paper)** projects perturbations onto the **null space** of preserved-knowledge key matrices; it "preserves 98.48% of its general ability on average across various tasks, even after 3,000 edits" and boosts locate-then-edit baselines "by an average of 36.7%," making it the current strongest sequential-editing approach and a plug-and-play enhancement to ROME/MEMIT. **MEMOIR (NeurIPS 2025)** ("Minimal Overwrite and Informed Retention," evaluated on LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct/ZsRE) and neuron-level/MoE-adaptor methods (LEMoE, NSE, SCAN) target the same problem.
- **Ripple effects & evaluation:** **Cohen et al., "Evaluating the Ripple Effects of Knowledge Editing" (RippleEdits, TACL 2024)** — a 5K-edit diagnostic benchmark across logical generalization, compositionality I/II, subject aliasing, preservation, and relation specificity — showed editing methods "fail to introduce consistent changes," and notably found that a **simple in-context editing baseline beat all parameter-editing methods**. **MQuAKE (Zhong et al.)** tests multi-hop post-edit reasoning; **KnowEdit (Zhang et al.)** is the standard composite benchmark. Editing accuracy on logical generalization can be as low as ~20%.
- **New paradigms beyond ROME/MEMIT:** circuit-aware editing (CaKE, arXiv 2503.16356), and a growing argument that the future is **in-context / retrieval-based knowledge updating rather than parameter editing** (e.g., "Knowledge Updating? No More Model Editing! Just Selective Contextual Reasoning," arXiv 2503.05212), which connects Thread 3 back to Thread 2 and Thread 4.

**Open problems:** the four evaluation axes — reliability, generalization, **locality**, and **portability** (downstream/reversed relations); whether parameter editing can ever be safe at lifelong scale; sequential-editing regularization. **Leading groups:** Mitchell / Manning / Finn (Stanford), Meng / Bau (Northeastern/MIT), Geva / Globerson (Tel Aviv), Ningyu Zhang / Huajun Chen (Zhejiang, EasyEdit toolkit), Anumanchipalli / Gupta (Berkeley).

### Thread 4 — Agent Memory (External, Often Model-Agnostic Memory Systems)

**Key recent systems.**
- **MemGPT (Packer et al., arXiv 2310.08560, 2023; now Letta)** introduced an **OS-inspired** virtual-context architecture: a two-tier memory (in-context "main memory" + external recall/archival storage) that the LLM pages in/out via function calls (`core_memory_append`, `archival_memory_search`).
- **Generative Agents (Park et al., UIST 2023)** introduced the **memory stream** with **recency, importance, and relevance** scoring plus **reflection** (synthesizing higher-level insights). Ablating reflection degraded coherent behavior within ~48 simulated hours.
- **MemoryBank (Zhong et al., AAAI 2024)** added **Ebbinghaus forgetting-curve** dynamics and user-profile construction.
- **Mem0 & Mem0g (Chhikara et al., arXiv 2504.19413, 2025; ECAI 2025)** is a production-grade memory layer that extracts/consolidates salient facts via ADD/UPDATE/DELETE/NOOP operations; Mem0g adds a Neo4j graph variant. On LoCoMo it reports "a 26% relative uplift in overall LLM-as-a-Judge score over OpenAI's memory feature — **66.9% versus 52.9%**," a "**91% reduction in p95 total latency (1.44s vs 17.12s** full-context)," and token savings exceeding 90% versus full-context.
- **A-MEM (Xu, Liang, Mei, Gao, Tan, Zhang; NeurIPS 2025; arXiv 2502.12110)** applies the **Zettelkasten** method — memories as atomic notes that dynamically generate descriptions, form links, and evolve into a "living" knowledge graph; agency at the level of memory *organization*, not just retrieval.

**Latest 2025–2026 work.**
- **RL-based memory management:** **Memory-R1 (Yan et al., arXiv 2508.19828, 2025)** trains a Memory Manager (ADD/UPDATE/DELETE/NOOP) and an Answer Agent with PPO/GRPO; "with only 152 training QA pairs, Memory-R1 outperforms strong baselines and generalizes across diverse question types, three benchmarks (LoCoMo, MSC, LongMemEval), and multiple model scales (3B–14B)," with its GRPO variant on LLaMA-3.1-8B achieving relative improvements of "28% in F1, 34% in BLEU-1, and 30% in LLM-as-a-Judge" over Mem0. **Mem-α** and **MemAgent** extend RL-based memory construction; **Mem1** optimizes multi-turn retrieval.
- **Hierarchical/graph memory:** Zep (temporal knowledge graph, arXiv 2501.13956), G-Memory (multi-agent hierarchical), H-MEM (semantic-abstraction levels), MemoryOS (three-tier OS-style), MIRIX (core/episodic/semantic/procedural/resource/vault), HippoRAG (spreading activation).
- **Reflective memory management & governance:** MemR³ (reflective retrieval, arXiv 2512.20237), SSGM (stability/safety-governed memory addressing poisoning/drift/hallucination, arXiv 2603.11768), memory-security surveys ("Mnemonic Sovereignty," arXiv 2604.16548).
- **Surveys:** **Zhang et al., "A Survey on the Memory Mechanism of LLM-based Agents" (ACM TOIS 2025, vol. 43(6); arXiv 2404.13501)** organizes memory by representation, source, and operation. **Du, "Memory for Autonomous LLM Agents: Mechanisms, Evaluation, and Emerging Frontiers" (arXiv 2603.07670, 2026)** formalizes a **write–manage–read loop** in a POMDP framing, with a three-axis taxonomy (temporal scope: working/episodic/semantic/procedural; representational substrate: context-resident/vector/structured/executable/hybrid; control policy: heuristic/prompted/learned) and reports MemoryArena dropping task completion from >80% to ~45% when swapping an active-memory agent for long-context-only. **"Memory in the Age of AI Agents" (Hu et al., arXiv 2512.13564, 2025)** is another major synthesis.
- **Benchmarks:** **LoCoMo, "Evaluating Very Long-Term Conversational Memory of LLM Agents" (Maharana, Lee, Tulyakov, Bansal, Barbieri, Fang; ACL 2024; arXiv 2402.17753)** is the canonical very-long-term conversational memory benchmark, built via a machine-human persona/temporal-event-graph pipeline; it found long-context LLMs and RAG improve "memory" by 22–66% but still lag humans by ~56% overall and ~73% on temporal reasoning. **LongMemEval**, **MemBench (Tan et al. 2025, arXiv 2506.21605)**, **MemoryAgentBench**, and **MemoryArena (2026)** are successors testing knowledge updates, multi-session recall, and memory-action coupling.

**Open problems:** episodic→semantic **consolidation** (largely heuristic today), **learned forgetting**, causally-grounded retrieval, contradiction handling, summarization drift, latency/privacy governance, multimodal embodied memory. **Leading groups:** Letta/Berkeley (Packer, Gonzalez), Stanford HCI (Park, Bernstein), Rutgers (Yongfeng Zhang, Xu), Mem0.ai, Renmin/Gaoling (Zhang, Wen), LangChain (LangMem).

### Cross-Cutting: The Long-Context-vs-RAG Debate

For ~6 months in late-2024/early-2025, the "RAG is obsolete, just paste everything" thesis spread as Claude Sonnet (1M tokens), Gemini 2.5 Pro (2M), and Llama 4 Scout (10M theoretical) shipped. The empirical verdict in 2025–2026 is that **long context does not obviate retrieval**:
- **"Lost in the Middle" (Nelson F. Liu, Kevin Lin, John Hewitt, Ashwin Paranjape, Michele Bevilacqua, Fabio Petroni, Percy Liang; TACL 2024, vol. 12, pp. 157–173; arXiv 2307.03172)** established the U-shaped position curve: "performance is often highest when relevant information occurs at the beginning or end... and significantly degrades when models must access relevant information in the middle," with a >30% accuracy drop when the gold document moved from position 1 to position 10 of 20. RoPE's long-term decay is one architectural cause.
- **"Context Rot: How Increasing Input Tokens Impacts LLM Performance" (Kelly Hong, Anton Troynikov, Jeff Huber; Chroma Research, July 2025)** tested 18 frontier models (incl. GPT-4.1, Claude 4, Gemini 2.5, Qwen3) and found "even under these minimal conditions, model performance degrades as input length increases, often in surprising and non-uniform ways" — sometimes 30–50% **well before** the documented window limit, and (counterintuitively) coherent/structured input degrades attention *more* than shuffled input. Semantic similarity decay matters more than raw length.
- **Production economics:** practitioner analyses report a 1M-token request running ~30–60× slower at ~1,250× the per-query cost of a RAG pipeline.
- **Emerging consensus (2026):** **hybrid is the default** — retrieve ~50K–200K relevant tokens, then long-context-reason over them; route simple queries to RAG and complex global queries to long context (Self-Route). RAG is being recast as a "context engine" rather than a doomed pattern. Beware the **over-fetching antipattern** (retrieving 30 chunks because "there's room"), which reintroduces context rot.

### Implications for Portable, Model-Agnostic Memory (Claude / Gemini / Cursor)

Several structural facts make **natural-language (and structured-text) memory stores more portable than raw-embedding stores**:
1. **Embeddings are model-specific.** A vector produced by one embedding model is not interpretable by another; switching LLM/embedding providers invalidates the index. Natural-language memories are universal — any model can read them, and they can be re-embedded on demand for whatever retrieval stack is in use. The agent-memory surveys explicitly note text/structured stores preserve relationships and transparency that vector stores lose.
2. **Weight-level approaches (Threads 1 & 3) are inherently non-portable.** Knowledge editing modifies a specific model's parameters; it cannot transfer across Claude/Gemini/Cursor and fails at lifelong scale anyway. This is a strong argument for keeping durable memory *external and textual*.
3. **MCP (Model Context Protocol)** — Anthropic's open, JSON-RPC, **model-agnostic** standard (Nov 2024) — has become the cross-industry integration layer, adopted by OpenAI, Google, Cursor, and Cline through 2025–2026. MCP cleanly separates the LLM host from tool/data servers (Tools, Resources, Prompts primitives), letting a single memory server back any compliant client. "Memory" is now a recognized MCP server category for persistent cross-session storage. The 2026 MCP roadmap is tackling stateful-session scaling, task lifecycles, and governance.
4. **Design recommendation that follows:** store memories as human-readable atomic notes/facts (A-MEM/Mem0 style) with optional graph structure, expose them via an MCP server, and re-embed for retrieval locally — yielding a memory layer portable across models and clients, auditable, and editable, while sidestepping both raw-embedding lock-in and weight-editing fragility.

## Recommendations
1. **For building a cross-platform memory product now:** adopt the **external natural-language store + MCP server** pattern. Use Mem0/A-MEM-style extract-consolidate pipelines for the write path and hybrid retrieval (BM25 + dense + optional late-interaction reranking) for the read path. Keep embeddings as a *disposable local index*, never the source of truth. **Threshold to revisit:** if a single target model and a stable embedding provider are guaranteed, a raw-vector store becomes acceptable.
2. **For retrieval architecture:** default to **hybrid (retrieve-then-long-context-reason)**, not pure long context. Cap retrieved context to high-precision top-k; resist over-fetching. Add a self-correction/reranking layer (CRAG/Self-RAG-style) for high-stakes queries. **Threshold:** if your corpus fits comfortably under ~50K tokens and queries are global/sensemaking, full-context (or GraphRAG) may win.
3. **For knowledge updating:** prefer **in-context / retrieval-based updating over parameter editing** for anything requiring durability or portability. Reserve weight editing (AlphaEdit if you must) for controlled, single-model, bounded-edit-count research or safety-scrubbing use cases — and benchmark on RippleEdits/KnowEdit, not just rewrite success. **Threshold:** abandon sequential parameter editing if forgetting on held-out edits exceeds your tolerance after a few hundred edits.
4. **For evaluation:** test agent memory on **LoCoMo + LongMemEval + a memory-action benchmark (MemBench/MemoryArena)**; report latency and token cost alongside accuracy, and specifically probe temporal reasoning and knowledge-update (contradiction) handling, where systems lag humans most.
5. **Watch items that would change the above:** a genuine breakthrough in lifelong editing without forgetting; embedding-space standardization (which would reduce the portability penalty of vector stores); and long-context architectures that demonstrably defeat context rot on adversarial mid-context benchmarks.

## Caveats
- **Settled vs. unsettled:** Foundations are settled in all four threads (the canonical papers above are stable). The **least settled** areas are (a) whether facts are meaningfully localizable and whether interpretability yields editable mechanisms; (b) lifelong editing without collapse; (c) episodic→semantic consolidation and learned forgetting in agent memory. The **most settled** is the long-context-vs-RAG question, where hybrid has clearly won.
- Several cited 2026 items (e.g., the Du survey arXiv 2603.07670, "Memory in the Age of AI Agents" 2512.13564, and various 26xx arXiv IDs) are very recent preprints; treat specific numbers as preliminary and not yet peer-reviewed.
- Some quantitative claims (e.g., the ~1,250× cost / 30–60× latency figures, the "98.48% general ability after 3,000 edits," HippoRAG's "up to 20%") come from individual papers or practitioner analyses and reflect specific models/setups; they are directionally robust but not universal constants.
- LoCoMo dataset statistics differ across sources: the ACL abstract cites ~300 turns/9K tokens/up to 35 sessions, while the released dataset and downstream papers cite larger per-conversation figures (~600 turns/~16K tokens) and ~10 conversations total — likely reflecting version updates; verify against the snap-research.github.io/locomo project page if exact numbers matter. GraphRAG's author list and HippoRAG's "10–20× vs 10–30× cheaper" wording also vary across arXiv versions.
- Vendor/marketing sources (Mem0.ai, RAGFlow, practitioner blogs) were used for trend framing only; all paper-level claims are anchored to arXiv/ACL/NeurIPS/ICLR primary sources.
